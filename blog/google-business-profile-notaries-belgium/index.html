Google Business Profile for Notaries in Belgium: What's Allowed, What Helps

Published July 25, 2026 · Acta by Found

Short answer: yes, a Belgian notary office can and should set up and optimize a Google Business Profile. It's treated as informational listing data — hours, address, services — rather than promotional advertising, which fits comfortably within Fednot's restrained communication norms. What changes for a notary versus a typical business is how you handle reviews and what tone the content takes — informative, not sales-driven.

Why a Google Business Profile matters for local search

When someone searches "notaire" or "notaris" plus a city name, Google shows a map with a handful of local listings above the regular search results — the "local pack." A complete, active Google Business Profile is one of the biggest factors in whether your office appears there. It's free, and for a notary office serving a specific area, it's often more directly tied to new client calls than the website itself.

What's clearly fine to include

  • Business name, address, and phone number — matching your website exactly
  • Accurate opening hours, including holiday exceptions
  • Your notarial acts listed as services (property transactions, wills, marriage contracts, company formation, and so on)
  • A factual business description of your office and what it handles
  • Photos of your office, team, and signage
  • Informational posts — office closures, new team members, general notices

What needs more care than a typical business

Reviews are the main area where notaries need to think differently than a restaurant or a shop. Client matters are often confidential and sensitive, so soliciting reviews right after a specific transaction risks implying what that client used your services for. A general, low-pressure request for feedback on service and communication — not case details — is the safer approach. Confirm current specifics with Fednot, since guidance can evolve.

The same applies to how you frame your description and posts: keep them informative rather than promotional. "We handle property transactions across [city]" reads very differently from "Belgium's #1 notary — book now," and only one of those fits the register Fednot expects.

A basic setup checklist

  • Claim and verify your profile if you haven't already (Google will mail a verification code to your office)
  • Fill in every available field — profiles with complete information rank and convert better than sparse ones
  • Use your actual office address, not a P.O. box, and keep it identical across your website and profile
  • Add at least 5-10 real photos of your office
  • Check the profile monthly for accuracy, especially around holiday hours

Related Questions

Can a notary office collect Google reviews?

Generally yes, but it needs more care than a typical business. Avoid soliciting reviews right after a specific transaction in a way that could reveal a client used your services for a sensitive matter, and never ask clients to reference case details in a review. A general, low-pressure request for feedback on service and communication is the safer approach — confirm current specifics with Fednot.

Does a Google Business Profile count as advertising under Fednot rules?

A Google Business Profile is generally treated as informational listing data — hours, address, services, contact details — rather than promotional advertising, which is why it sits more comfortably within Fednot's restrained communication norms than a typical marketing channel. The content you add to it should stay factual and informative rather than sales-driven, consistent with those norms.

What should a notary's Google Business Profile include?

Accurate business name, address, and phone number matching your website exactly, correct opening hours, your notarial acts listed as services, a factual business description, and photos of your office. Keep posts and updates informational — office hours changes, new team members, general notices — rather than promotional.
